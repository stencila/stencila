Abstract

Nine small felid species, including the introduced domestic cat, inhabit Southeast Asia. We analysed their skull morphology, using 36 selected morphometric measurements of up to 465 specimens. Of the species examined, the fishing cat and the Asian golden cat were the largest, whilst the mainland and Sunda leopard cats were the smallest. However, there was substantial overlap in size amongst these species. Principal Component Analysis and Quadratic Discriminant Analysis revealed that the skulls of fishing cats and flat-headed cats were clearly distinguishable from those of the other species. Additionally, the skulls of male Asian golden cats, female jungle cats, female Sunda leopard cats, and male marbled cats were well differentiated. In contrast, those of female Asian golden cats, male jungle cats, male domestic cats, and female marbled cats showed considerable morphological overlap with other morphospecies. When analysing only sympatric assemblages (i.e., on the continental mainland, Borneo, Sumatra, and Java), each morphospecies was generally well differentiated from the others although conspecific males and females were not clearly differentiated. Notable exceptions were female Asian golden cats and male jungle cats, female mainland leopard cats and female domestic cats, and female marbled cats and male domestic cats on the mainland, and female marbled cats and male domestic cats on Borneo and Sumatra. More detailed research is necessary to understand the impacts of introduced domestic cats on indigenous small wild felids in Southeast Asia through niche overlap and competition.

Introduction

Southeast Asia, renowned for its exceptionally rich biodiversity, is home to eight indigenous small felid species, which are the Asian golden cat (Catopuma temminckii), bay cat (C. badia), fishing cat (Prionailurus viverrinus), mainland leopard cat (P. bengalensis), Sunda leopard cat (P. javanensis), flat-headed cat (P. planiceps), marbled cat (Pardofelis marmorata), and jungle cat (Felis chaus) (Fig. S1)1,2. Amongst them, mainland leopard cat and Sunda leopard cat, which had hitherto been considered as subspecies of the former leopard cat (P. bengalensis), were only recently suggested to be two separate species phylogenetically based on mitochondrial, X chromosome, and Y chromosome DNAs, with a possible hybrid zone on the Malay Peninsula2–4. In addition to these wild species, the introduced domestic cat (F. catus) is widespread in the region (Fig. S1)5–7. These species were largely sympatric, on either the mainland (Asian golden cat, fishing cat, mainland leopard cat, flat-headed cat, marbled cat, jungle cat, and domestic cat) or the Sunda Islands (bay cat, marbled cat, flat-headed cat, Sunda leopard cat, and domestic cat on Borneo, Asian golden cat, marbled cat, flat-headed cat, Sunda leopard cat, and domestic cat on Sumatra, and fishing cat, Sunda leopard cat, and domestic cat on Java, although the fishing cat may be extinct on Java) until recent anthropogenic fragmentations of their ranges2,8. Therefore, they provide valuable model communities of closely-related obligate carnivores for studying niche partitioning. Furthermore, due to their charisma and popularity, they attract the interest of both researchers and the general public1,8–10; this is particularly the case concerning the conservation of the fishing cat, Asian golden cat (both are classified as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List), flat-headed cat (Endangered), and bay cat (Endangered and endemic to Borneo)11–14. However, compared to large felids in the region (tiger (Panthera tigris), leopard (P. pardus), mainland clouded leopard (Neofelis nebulosa), and Sunda clouded leopard (N. diardi)), even the basic biology of these smaller species remains little known2,8,15.

Studies on niche partitioning amongst small felids in Southeast Asia have primarily relied on modelling based on data collected through camera trapping1,16–19, with only a few dietary analyses conducted in the region20–22. However, due to anthropogenic range/habitat fragmentation and associated faunal changes, the opportunity may have passed for collecting field data indicative of pre-fragmentation range overlap, and hence, niche partitioning in an evolutionary context. However, it may be possible to infer something of these relationships from morphological analysis, which may also shed light on the impact of niche overlap with the introduced domestic cat on indigenous wild felids.

Sympatric carnivorans are considered to differ from each other in distinct ways, including morphological differences, so as to reduce competition amongst species, as well as between sexes23–25. Skull morphology is a valuable tool for assessing niche overlap amongst sympatric and closely related carnivorans25–28. Some previous studies have highlighted possible character displacement and range overlap amongst “morphospecies” (i.e. male and female are different morphospecies where clear sexual dimorphism occurs) in felids by focusing on size differences of skull, canine, and carnassial24,29,30. Previous studies of the skull morphology of Southeast Asian small felids have been limited by small sample sizes and have not supported statistical analysis of niche overlap amongst morphospecies27,31,32. A comparison of skull morphology of Southeast Asian small felids, not only species but also morphospecies, based on a large dataset, could significantly enhance understanding of the morphological evidence for niche partitioning amongst them.

To address this knowledge gap, we analyse the skull morphology of all nine small felid species in Southeast Asia, including the domestic cat, using arguably the largest single dataset in existence. We investigate the extent of skull morphology overlap amongst sympatric species/morphospecies as a proxy for niche overlap. Our hypothesis is that sympatric small cat species/morphospecies are differentiated based on skull morphology, following the suggestion that sympatric carnivorans are likely to have evolved in ways that reduce competition.

Results

Size differences in skull, canine, and carnassial measurements amongst species

In all nine species examined, males were significantly larger than females in terms of greatest skull length, anteroposterior diameter of the upper canine, and length of the upper carnassial (Pm4) (Table S1). Amongst these species, the fishing cat and Asian golden cat were the largest, whilst the mainland leopard cat, Sunda leopard cat, and domestic cat were the smallest. However, considerable overlap was observed in these three morphological variables between morphospecies within similar size ranges (Fig. S2).

Differentiation of skull morphology amongst species

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on 36 cranial variables from 373 skulls extracted two principal components (PCs) with eigenvalues greater than one, explaining 86.7% and 3.9% of the total variance, respectively. Biplots suggested mandible length (coronoid process), lateral snout, and greatest length were the top three variables most strongly influencing PC1 whilst postorbital breadth, interorbital breadth, and brain case were the top three for PC2 (Fig. S3). Scatterplots of these two PCs revealed clear morphological differentiation of fishing cats and flat-headed cats from the other species. In contrast, extensive overlap was observed between the skulls of mainland and Sunda leopard cats, which also partially overlapped with those of domestic cats (Fig. 1). Partial overlap was found amongst the skulls of domestic cats, marbled cats, bay cats, and jungle cats, as well as between jungle cats and Asian golden cats (Fig. 1). Quadratic Discriminant Analysis (QDA), conducted on eight species (excluding the bay cat due to a limited sample size, n = 2), extracted two discriminant functions (DFs) that explained 95.4% of the variance. At the species level 81.4% of skulls were correctly classified (Table 1). The classification results (Table 1) indicated that fishing cats and flat-headed cats were most clearly distinguished from the other species, while the remaining species showed less clear separation.

Differentiation of skull morphology amongst morphospecies

Principal Component Analysis conducted on 36 cranial variables from 331 skulls, representing 16 morphospecies (eight species × two sexes), excluding the bay cat (with only one sexed specimen), extracted two PCs with eigenvalues greater than one, explaining 86.9% and 3.8% of the variance, respectively. Scatterplots based on these PCs indicated that fishing cats and flat-headed cats were distinctly separated from the other morphospecies, although partial overlap occurred between sexes within each species (Fig. 2).

Conversely, skulls of mainland and Sunda leopard cats overlapped extensively, regardless of sex (Fig. 2). Domestic cat skull shapes did not overlap with those of the leopard cats as much as they did with unsexed specimens being included in this analysis (cf. Fig. 1). Skulls of domestic cats overlapped with those of female marbled cats, whereas male marbled cats were largely distinct from all other morphospecies (Fig. 2). Asian golden cats and jungle cats exhibited overlapping skull morphologies, particularly between female Asian golden cats and male jungle cats, whilst male Asian golden cats and female jungle cats showed no overlap (Fig. 2). Quadratic Discriminant Analysis on the 16 morphospecies (excluding bay cat) extracted two DFs explaining 92.1% of the variance, with 68.0% of skulls correctly classified (Table 2). The classification results (Table 2) showed that both male and female fishing cats and flat-headed cats were well separated from other morphospecies, with over 80.0% correctly classified for each group. Additionally, skulls of male Asian golden cats, female jungle cats, female Sunda leopard cats, and male marbled cats were also well differentiated (> 81.3% correct classification; Table 2). However, lower classification accuracy (< 42.1%) was observed for female Asian golden cats, male jungle cats, male domestic cats, and female marbled cats (Table 2). When considering geographical sympatry (i.e., mainland, Borneo, Sumatra, and Java), morphospecies generally overlapped only with the opposite sex of the same species (Tables S2–S5). Notable exceptions involving interspecific overlap were between female Asian golden cat and male jungle cat, female mainland leopard cat and female domestic cat, and female marbled cat and male domestic cat on the mainland, as well as female marbled cat and male domestic cat on Borneo and Sumatra (Tables S2–S5).

Discussion

Differentiating species/morphospecies based on skull morphology

In general, the results suggest that in general Southeast Asian small felids – both species and morphospecies – can be differentiated using QDA consistent with the hypothesis that sympatric felids have evolved to avoid competition. The results also suggest that variables related to the overall size of the skull, including mandible length (coronoid process), lateral snout, and greatest length, and those related to skull breadth, postorbital breadth, interorbital breadth, and brain case, might be useful for differentiating the species (Fig. S3). However, sexual dimorphism is not clearly reflected in most species, as conspecific males and females are not distinctly separated (Tables 1 and 2). Amongst the species, the fishing cat and flat-headed cat are most distinctly differentiated from the other species (Table 1). At the morphospecies level, male marbled cats are clearly differentiated from all others (Table 2). Female marbled cats would also be well distinguished from other indigenous morphospecies, were it not for the inclusion of domestic cats in the analysis (Table 2). Mainland and Sunda leopard cats are not clearly differentiated and yet this is expected as they likely fill the same ecological niche although they were recently separated into two species phylogenetically. Unless clear evidence would suggest otherwise, it is safe to assume that mainland leopard cat and Sunda leopard cat form a single ecological species, and two morphospecies (i.e. male and female leopard cats), in their hybrid zone in the Malay Peninsula3,4. Notably, an overlap is observed between female Asian golden cats and male jungle cats (Table 2). Additionally, overlap is evident between mainland leopard cats and domestic cats, especially between females (Table 2), as well as between marbled cats and domestic cats to a lesser extent (again, particularly between females). We tentatively suggest that the bay cat may overlap, in terms of skull morphology, with the jungle cat, marbled cat, and domestic cat (Fig. 1). When only actual sympatric distributions are considered (i.e., mainland Southeast Asia, Borneo, Sumatra, and Java), the only notable overlap between indigenous morphospecies occurs between female Asian golden cats and male jungle cats. All other significant overlaps involve the domestic cat (Tables S2–S5).

Deduced niche overlap

Overall, the diet of all small felids worldwide is composed of rodents and other small vertebrates, and the general predation strategy is similar27. Consequently, some degree of interspecific competition is expected amongst sympatric species. The central assumption of our study is that greater similarity in skull morphology implies greater ecological niche overlap and thus increased potential for competition, assuming other factors remain constant. Based on this assumption, the fishing cat and flat-headed cat may occupy distinct ecological niches, with minimal overlap with other regional felids. This is likely due to their specialised habitat preferences (riparian environments) and diet (predominantly fish) (Table S6)11,13, which differ from those of the other species, and the size difference between the two species (Fig. S2) separating their niches. However, it is important to note that morphological differentiation does not necessarily preclude competition (and antagonistic interactions) in areas of sympatry. Owing to the paucity of information concerning the behaviour and ecology of the marbled cat, we cannot suggest a convincing adaptive reason for male marbled cats being clearly differentiated from all other morphospecies. However, the possible arboreal lifestyle of the marbled cat and the possibility of hunting relatively large prey for its body size33,34 may be relevant. On the other hand, the highest potential for niche overlap amongst sympatric species or morphospecies appears to occur between female Asian golden cats and male jungle cats, female mainland leopard cats and female domestic cats, and female marbled cats and domestic cats.

Asian golden cat and jungle cat

The results suggest that, based on skull morphology, the niche overlap between the Asian golden cat and jungle cat – particularly between female Asian golden cats and male jungle cats – is amongst the greatest observed in Southeast Asian small felids. Consequently, competition between these two species, where they are sympatric, may be more intense compared to other small felid species in the region. However, the Asian golden cat is strongly associated with closed-canopy forests, habitats in which the jungle cat may not occur (Table S6)14,35. This difference in habitat preference may limit the extent of their sympatric range in Southeast Asia. The degree of polymorphism within Asian golden cat populations varies with latitude, being lowest in Peninsular Malaysia and Sumatra. This has led to the hypothesis of a southward population expansion of the Asian golden cat during the Late Pleistocene/Early Holocene36,37. In contrast, there is limited information regarding the colonisation of Southeast Asia by the jungle cat30. As such, it remains difficult to deduce the recent evolutionary histories of the two species, particularly in relation to their colonisation of, and competition in, Southeast Asia. Notably, jungle cats in the eastern part of their range, including Southeast Asia, are substantially smaller than those in the western part, and this size difference has been suggested to result from competition with sympatric small felids30. Our analysis, which includes jungle cats from across the species’ range, shows that their average size is slightly smaller than that of the Asian golden cat (Fig. S2). In this context, the smaller size of jungle cats in Southeast Asia may help reducing niche overlap and competition with the Asian golden cat in the region. The indigenous small felids in Southeast Asia smaller than the jungle cat, which are the flat-headed cat (a riparian specialist), the marbled cat (possibly arboreal), and the mainland leopard cat, may exert limited competition. Amongst these species, only the mainland leopard cat might compete with the jungle cat, yet it is substantially smaller (Fig. S2), potentially allowing the jungle cat to have evolved a smaller size to alleviate competition with the Asian golden cat.

Niche overlap between Indigenous small felids and domestic Cat

Free-ranging domestic cats pose significant conservation concerns worldwide38,39. However, there have been few studies on their impact on indigenous Southeast Asian small felids through niche overlap and competition. Our results indicate that when considering actual sympatry, with the exception of the female Asian golden cat and male jungle cat, all other dyads showing notable overlap involve the domestic cat (Tables S2-S5). It may be possible to suggest that the introduced domestic cat may not have been sympatric with other small felids in Southeast Asia long enough for evolutionary processes to reduce niche overlap, unlike in the case of indigenous species. The results suggest that potential niche overlap is greater between the domestic cat and the female mainland leopard cat (particularly with female domestic cats), as well as with the female marbled cat, compared to other dyads of small felids in Southeast Asia. The marbled cat’s putative arboreal lifestyle and its predation of relatively large arboreal prey for its body size33,34 may help to reduce competition between female marbled cats and domestic cats. However, due to the limited knowledge regarding the behaviour and ecology of marbled cats33,34, it may be a premature conclusion that the negative impact of free-ranging domestic cats on marbled cats is minimal where they are sympatric. Similar caution is necessary regarding the interaction between domestic cats and mainland leopard cats. A study on the distribution of faeces suggests a possible difference in spatial use between free-ranging domestic cats and Iriomote cats (an island population of the mainland leopard cat on Iriomote Island, Japan), with domestic cats being more associated with human settlements, particularly garbage dumps40. A survey on Bohol Island, Philippines where there is no leopard cat, found that feral domestic cats were not recorded in primary forests5, suggesting that competition between mainland leopard cats and domestic cats may be reduced, at least in forested areas. However, leopard cats also inhabit areas outside primary forest habitats (Table S6), where they may be sympatric with free-ranging domestic cats. A study in Singapore showed that leopard cats and domestic cats had overlapping ranges in an oil palm plantation near secondary forest edges41. Free-ranging domestic cats exploit a wide range of habitats, including various forest and woodland types42. It is believed that habitat use by free-ranging domestic cats is influenced by factors such as prey availability, predation/competition, shelter availability, and human resource subsidies, although the evidence supporting these assertions remains weak, with most studies being observational or correlative42. Furthermore, the impact of domestic cats on indigenous small felids through niche overlap and competition is understudied, with Southeast Asia being a notably underrepresented region in terms of research on the effects of domestic cats on wildlife conservation39. Although hybridisation between free-ranging domestic cats and indigenous small wild felids has not been documented in Southeast Asia, domestic cat is known to hybridise with many different cat species39,43–45, and it may be worth investigating whether or not such hybridisation occurs in the region. Further research will be necessary to understand the impact of the introduced domestic cat on indigenous small wild felids in Southeast Asia.

Materials and methods

Data collection

We examined a total of 438 skulls from the eight wild small felid species found in Southeast Asia: Asian golden cat (Catopuma temminckii), bay cat (C. badia), marbled cat (Pardofelis marmorata), flat-headed cat (Prionailurus planiceps), mainland leopard cat (P. bengalensis), Sunda leopard cat (P. javanensis), fishing cat (P. viverrinus), and jungle cat (Felis chaus). Additionally, we analysed 27 skulls of the domestic cat (F. catus) obtained from natural history collections in Europe, North America, and Asia (see the Acknowledgements for the details). Specimen provenance was determined based on collection labels and associated records. We collected up to 56 morphometric measurements per skull, following a modified protocol based on ref.46 (Table S7, Fig. S4). All measurements were taken by the same measurer (NY) to minimise measurer-related differences. A skull was classified as juvenile if the cemento-enamel junction of any permanent canine was not clearly visible above its alveolus. If all the cemento-enamel junctions were visible above the alveoli, a skull was classified as sub-adult if the basioccipital-basisphenoid suture was still open, or adult if the suture was closed. Sex was determined using museum labels when available, and, in some obvious cases with missing information, was inferred using sexual size dimorphism, especially in canine size (ref.47, but also see ref. 32). Additionally, we assessed whether specimens were wild or captive based on museum records.

Data analysis

Previous studies have highlighted possible character displacement and range overlap amongst morphospecies in felids by focusing on size differences of skull, canine, and carnassial24,29,30. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to detect significant difference in greatest length of skull, antero-posterior breadth of upper canine, and length of upper carnassial (Pm4), between species and sexes. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and Discriminant Analysis (DA) were carried out to assess skull morphology overlap and differentiation amongst species and sexes. Wild and captive animals are known to differ in their skull morphology in lion and tiger48–50. It may be safe to assume the similar difference in small felids, which may have influenced our results. However, because we did not detect any substantial difference in the scatter plots, based on Principal Components (PCs) 1 and 2 between all animals pooled and wild animals only (Figs. S5 & S6), we pooled both wild and captive animals together to increase the sample size. Similarly, the dates of the specimens we analysed ranged between 1819 and 2012 during which substantial (anthropogenic) environmental changes have occurred in the region, and the skull morphology of wild cats may have changed. The scatter plots between pre-1950 and post-1950, based on Principal Component (PC) 1 and 2, may tentatively suggest that skull morphology may have shifted between the two periods in fishing cat, Sunda leopard cat, marbled cat, and domestic cat (Figs. S7-S14). However, because there were extensive overlaps in skull morphology between the two periods in general, even in these four species, we pooled all animals together for the analysis regardless of their dates, for increasing the sample size. We included adult and sub-adult skulls. To increase sample sizes for certain species, we incorporated specimens collected outside Southeast Asia, particularly for the mainland leopard cat, jungle cat, and domestic cat, as their ranges extend beyond the region. Some skulls were damaged, limiting the number of variables measured. Consequently, we analysed a dataset that maximised the sample size whilst minimising the number of excluded variables, resulting in datasets containing 36 variables for 373 skulls including 185 males and 148 females (Tables S8 & S9). Based on mitochondrial, X chromosome, and Y chromosome DNAs, it is possible that phylogenetic Sunda leopard cats occur in the Malay Peninsula alongside phylogenetic mainland leopard cats3,4. However, due to (1) lack of detailed phylogenetic information of each specimen and (2) mainland and Sunda leopard cats likely fill the same ecological niche, we classified all the leopard cat specimens from the Malay Peninsular as the mainland leopard cat. Discriminant Analysis is more statistically appropriate when applied to a reduced set of principal components (PCs) extracted through PCA rather than using the full set of original variables51, a methodology we followed. However, for each dataset analysed the Box’s M, which tests if the variance/covariance matrices are equal across two or more groups (one of the assumptions for linear DA), was highly statistically significant suggesting that the assumption is violated52. Consequently, we applied Quadratic DA instead53. All statistical analyses were conducted using SPSS 29 (IBM, Armonk) and/or R (https://www.r-project.org). The scripts for R that we used for PCA and QDA are found in the Supplementary Information.
